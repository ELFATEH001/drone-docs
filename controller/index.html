    Controller

    Overview

    The drone is an underactuated system: it has 4 control inputs (rotor speeds) but 6 degrees of freedom (3 translational, 3 rotational). As a result, it is impossible to independently control all positions and orientations simultaneously. Our control strategy therefore targets 4 degrees of freedom: the 3D position \(p^w\) and the yaw angle \(\psi\).

    Two control architectures were studied and implemented:

    Controller Approach Suitable for
    PD (near-hovering) Linearization around hover Small angle maneuvers
    Geometric SE(3) Nonlinear, SO(3)-based Aggressive maneuvers

    The geometric controller is the primary controller used in this project.

    Dynamic Model

    The quadrotor dynamics are described by the Newton-Euler equations for a rigid body:

    \[\begin{bmatrix} f^w \\ \tau^B \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} mI_3 & 0 \\ 0 & J \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a^w \\ \alpha^B \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -mg^w \\ \omega^B \times J\omega^B \end{bmatrix}\]

    Expanding with the forces and torques generated by the rotors, the full model becomes:

    \[\begin{bmatrix} m\dot{v}^w \\ J\dot{\omega}^B \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -mge_3 \\ -\omega^B \times J\omega^B \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} R^w_B & 0 \\ 0 & I_3 \end{bmatrix} F\mathbf{w}\]

    where \(\mathbf{w} = [w_1|w_1|,\ w_2|w_2|,\ w_3|w_3|,\ w_4|w_4|]^T\) contains the signed squared rotor velocities, and \(F\) is the allocation matrix mapping rotor inputs to forces and torques:

    \[\begin{bmatrix} f_x^B \\ f_y^B \\ f_z^B \\ \tau_x^B \\ \tau_y^B \\ \tau_z^B \end{bmatrix} = F\mathbf{w} = \begin{bmatrix} c_f \mathbf{e}_3 & c_f \mathbf{e}_3 & c_f \mathbf{e}_3 & c_f \mathbf{e}_3 \\ c_d \mathbf{e}_3 + c_f \boldsymbol{\rho}_1^B \times \mathbf{e}_3 & -c_d \mathbf{e}_3 + c_f \boldsymbol{\rho}_2^B \times \mathbf{e}_3 & c_d \mathbf{e}_3 + c_f \boldsymbol{\rho}_3^B \times \mathbf{e}_3 & -c_d \mathbf{e}_3 + c_f \boldsymbol{\rho}_4^B \times \mathbf{e}_3 \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{w}\]

    The state of the system is \((p^w,\ v^w,\ R^w_B,\ \omega^B)\) and evolves according to:

    \[\dot{p}^w = v^w, \qquad \dot{R}^w_B = R^w_B [\omega^B]_\times\]

    Geometric Controller on SE(3)

    The geometric controller was originally introduced by Lee, Leok, and McClamroch (2010). It is a nonlinear controller defined directly on the Lie group \(SE(3) = SO(3) \times \mathbb{R}^3\), which avoids the singularities associated with Euler angle representations (gimbal lock). Unlike PD or backstepping controllers, it supports aggressive maneuvers such as recovery from an inverted state.

    Control Architecture

    The controller takes as inputs the desired position \(p^w_d\) and a desired yaw direction \(\tilde{x}^w_d = [\cos\psi_d,\ \sin\psi_d,\ 0]^T\), and outputs the total thrust \(f^B_z\) and the control torque \(\tau^B\).

    Tracking Errors

    The errors are defined on the appropriate manifolds:

    \[e_p = p^w - p^w_d \qquad \text{(position error)}\]
    \[e_v = v^w - v^w_d \qquad \text{(velocity error)}\]
    \[e_R = \frac{1}{2}\left((R^w_d)^T R^w_B - (R^w_B)^T R^w_d\right)^\vee \qquad \text{(rotation error on SO(3))}\]
    \[e_\omega = \omega^B - (R^w_B)^T R^w_d\, \omega_d \qquad \text{(angular velocity error)}\]

    The rotation error \(e_R\) is a vector parametrization of the relative rotation between the current attitude \(R^w_B\) and the desired attitude \(R^w_d\). It is derived from the SO(3) log map and vanishes when the two frames are aligned.

    Desired Attitude Construction

    The desired third body axis \(z^w_d\) is constructed from the position controller output:

    \[z^w_d = \frac{-k_p e_p - k_v e_v + mge_3 + m\ddot{p}^w_d}{\lVert -k_p e_p - k_v e_v + mge_3 + m\ddot{p}^w_d \rVert}\]

    The full desired rotation matrix is then assembled as:

    \[R^w_d = \left[\frac{(z^w_d \times \tilde{x}^w_d) \times z^w_d}{\lVert(z^w_d \times \tilde{x}^w_d) \times z^w_d\rVert},\quad \frac{z^w_d \times \tilde{x}^w_d}{\lVert z^w_d \times \tilde{x}^w_d \rVert},\quad z^w_d\right] \in SO(3)\]

    Control Laws

    Thrust:

    \[f^B_z = \left(-k_p e_p - k_v e_v + mge_3 + m\ddot{p}^w_d\right) \cdot R^w_B e_3\]

    The dot product with \(R^w_B e_3\) projects the desired force onto the current body \(z\)-axis. When there is an attitude error, the thrust magnitude is naturally reduced, preventing instability.

    Torque:

    \[\tau^B = -k_R e_R - k_\omega e_\omega + \omega^B \times J\omega^B - J\left([\omega^B]_\times (R^w_B)^T R^w_d\, \omega_d - (R^w_B)^T R^w_d\, \dot{\omega}_d\right)\]

    This torque law exponentially stabilizes the attitude tracking error on \(SO(3)\).

    Gain Summary

    Gain Controls Effect
    \(k_p\) Position error \(e_p\) Stiffness of position response
    \(k_v\) Velocity error \(e_v\) Damping of translational dynamics
    \(k_R\) Rotation error \(e_R\) Stiffness of attitude response
    \(k_\omega\) Angular velocity error \(e_\omega\) Damping of rotational dynamics

    Implementation

    The geometric controller is implemented as a ROS 2 node (controller_node_visual) in C++, using the Eigen library for all linear algebra operations. It subscribes to:

    • /mavros/local_position/pose â€” current position and orientation (\(p^w\), \(R^w_B\))
    • /mavros/local_position/velocity_body â€” current body-frame velocity
    • Desired trajectory setpoints (position, velocity, acceleration, yaw)

    And publishes:

    • /mavros/setpoint_raw/attitude â€” attitude setpoint and thrust command sent to ArduPilot via MAVROS

    The allocation matrix inversion \(F^{-1}\) is handled internally by ArduPilot on the MiniPix flight controller.

    Comparison with PD Controller

    The near-hovering PD controller is derived by linearizing the equations of motion around the hover equilibrium (\(\phi = \theta = 0\), \(\dot{p} = 0\)). It uses nested control loops for attitude and position, with Euler angles as the attitude representation.

    While simple and computationally efficient, the PD controller:

    • Is only valid near hover (small roll and pitch angles)
    • Suffers from Euler angle singularities
    • Cannot perform aggressive maneuvers

    The geometric controller lifts these restrictions by working directly on \(SO(3)\), at the cost of slightly higher implementation complexity.
